Sample Episode 1: The Planner Paradox

📒 The Planner Paradox

⏱️ 12 minutes 🎯 Topic: Planning systems that actually work for ADHD 💡 Tool: The "Anti-Planner" method

🎣 Hook (0:00-1:00)

So let me guess. You bought a planner in January. It was beautiful—leather cover, color-coded sections, stickers galore. You spent an entire Sunday setting it up, filling out your goals, your habits, your "ideal week."

And now it's... March? And that planner is either collecting dust on your shelf or being used as a coaster for your coffee.

Welcome to the Planner Paradox. The more elaborate the planning system, the faster we abandon it. And today, we're going to talk about why that happens—and what actually works instead.

💚 Validation (1:00-2:00)

First, let me say this: You're not broken. You're not lazy. You don't lack discipline.

You have ADHD. And ADHD brains don't play by the same rules as neurotypical brains. The systems designed for them—the beautiful planners, the habit trackers, the "just do it" motivation—those weren't built for how YOUR brain works.

I've bought probably twenty planners in my life. I've abandoned nineteen of them. And the twentieth? I use it wrong. And that's okay.

🧠 Main Content (2:00-8:00)

Here's the thing about ADHD and planning: We love the IDEA of planning. The setup is exciting. The possibilities are endless. But the execution? That's where we fall apart.

Why? Because traditional planners assume you'll look at them every day. They assume you'll remember to check them. They assume you have a consistent routine.

ADHD brains don't do consistent. We do intense. We do passionate. We do "all-in for three days and then forget it exists."

So what works? Let me tell you about the Anti-Planner.

The Anti-Planner isn't a product you buy. It's a mindset shift. Instead of asking "How can I be more organized?" we ask "How can I make this easier for my brain?"

Here are three Anti-Planner principles:

One: Visibility beats beauty. Your system needs to be in your face. Sticky notes on the bathroom mirror. A whiteboard by the door. A phone wallpaper that says "Dentist at 2pm." If you have to remember to check a planner, you've already lost.

Two: One day at a time. Monthly spreads are overwhelming. Weekly spreads are overwhelming. Daily? That's manageable. What do I need to do TODAY? Just today. Not tomorrow. Not next week. Today.

Three: Done is better than perfect. A messy sticky note with your three priorities is better than a pristine planner you never open. A scribbled reminder on your hand is better than a forgotten appointment.

I have a friend who keeps a single index card in her pocket. That's her planner. One card. She writes three things on it every morning. When the card is full, she throws it away and starts a new one. That's it. That's her system. And it works because it's simple, visible, and low-pressure.

🛠️ The 2-Minute Tool (8:00-10:00)

Today's Tool: The "One Card" Method

Here's what you're going to do:

  1. Grab an index card, a sticky note, or even a scrap of paper.
  2. Write down THREE things you want to do today. Just three.
  3. Put that card somewhere you'll see it constantly—your pocket, your desk, taped to your monitor.
  4. When you do something, cross it off. Messily. With satisfaction.
  5. At the end of the day, throw it away. Or keep it if you want. But tomorrow starts fresh with a new card.

That's it. No fancy system. No elaborate setup. Just one card, three things, done.

🎬 Outro (10:00-12:00)

So here's my challenge to you: Try the One Card method for one week. Just one week. See how it feels.

And remember—if you miss a day, if you forget your card, if you write five things instead of three—it's fine. The goal isn't perfection. The goal is progress, however small.

You don't need a better planner. You need a system that works with your brain, not against it.

Thanks for listening to Focus & Flow. If this episode resonated with you, share it with someone who needs to hear it. And remember—small steps count. Progress is progress. You're doing better than you think.

Until next time, keep flowing.

Sample Episode 2: The Motivation Myth

⚡ The Motivation Myth

⏱️ 11 minutes 🎯 Topic: Why waiting for motivation doesn't work 💡 Tool: The 5-Minute Rule

🎣 Hook (0:00-1:00)

"I just need to get motivated."

How many times have you said that? I need to get motivated to clean my house. I need to get motivated to start that project. I need to get motivated to go to the gym.

Here's the truth that nobody tells you: Motivation is a myth. It's not real. At least, not the way we think about it.

And waiting for motivation? That's the best way to guarantee nothing gets done.

💚 Validation (1:00-2:00)

Let me be clear: This isn't your fault. We've been sold a lie our whole lives. "Just get motivated!" "Find your why!" "Visualize your goals!"

And when that doesn't work? We blame ourselves. We think we're lazy. We think we don't want it enough. We think something's wrong with us.

But here's what nobody tells you: ADHD brains don't produce dopamine the same way neurotypical brains do. Motivation isn't a choice for us. It's a neurochemical response that we can't just turn on.

🧠 Main Content (2:00-8:00)

So if motivation doesn't work, what does?

Action. Action creates motivation, not the other way around.

I know, I know. That sounds like toxic positivity. "Just do it!" But hear me out. I'm not talking about forcing yourself to work for hours. I'm talking about something much smaller.

I'm talking about five minutes.

Here's the thing: Starting is the hardest part. The gap between doing nothing and doing something feels enormous. But the gap between doing nothing and doing five minutes? That's manageable.

When you tell yourself "I need to clean the entire house," your brain goes NOPE. Too big. Too overwhelming. Can't do it.

But when you tell yourself "I'm going to clean for five minutes," your brain goes... okay. I can do five minutes. That's nothing. That's one song. That's a commercial break.

And here's the magic: Once you start, you usually keep going. Not because you found motivation, but because action creates momentum.

I've written entire articles that started with "I'll just open the document." I've cleaned entire rooms that started with "I'll just put away one shirt." I've gone on runs that started with "I'll just put on my shoes."

The five-minute rule isn't about what you accomplish in five minutes. It's about tricking your brain into starting.

And if you do five minutes and stop? That's fine too. You did five minutes. That's five minutes more than zero. That's progress.

🛠️ The 2-Minute Tool (8:00-10:00)

Today's Tool: The 5-Minute Rule

Next time you're staring at a task you don't want to do, try this:

  1. Set a timer for five minutes. Use your phone, your watch, whatever.
  2. Tell yourself: "I'm only doing this for five minutes. Then I can stop."
  3. Start the timer. Do the thing.
  4. When the timer goes off, you have permission to stop. Or keep going if you want.

That's it. No motivation required. Just five minutes.

Pro tip: Make the five minutes as easy as possible. If you're supposed to exercise, just put on your workout clothes. If you're supposed to write, just open the document. The goal isn't productivity. The goal is starting.

🎬 Outro (10:00-11:00)

Stop waiting for motivation. It's not coming. And that's okay.

You don't need to feel like doing something to do it. You just need to start. Five minutes. That's all.

Small steps count. Progress is progress. And you're doing better than you think.

Thanks for listening to Focus & Flow. Share this with someone who's waiting to "feel motivated." And remember—you don't need more motivation. You need smaller tasks.

Until next time, keep flowing.

Sample Episode 3: The Shame Spiral

🌀 The Shame Spiral

⏱️ 13 minutes 🎯 Topic: Breaking the cycle of ADHD shame 💡 Tool: The "Evidence File"

🎣 Hook (0:00-1:00)

You forgot to pay the bill. Again.

Now there's a late fee. And you're thinking: "I can't believe I did this again. I'm so irresponsible. Other people can handle basic adult tasks. What's wrong with me?"

Sound familiar?

That's the shame spiral. And if you have ADHD, you know it well. Today, we're talking about how to break it.

💚 Validation (1:00-2:00)

First, let me say this directly to you: You are not irresponsible. You are not broken. You are not a failure.

You have ADHD. Your brain works differently. And the world is not set up for brains like ours.

The shame you feel? It comes from trying to live by neurotypical rules in a neurotypical world. But those rules weren't made for you. And failing at someone else's game doesn't make you a failure.

🧠 Main Content (2:00-8:00)

Here's how the shame spiral works:

You forget something. You feel bad about forgetting it. You tell yourself you're irresponsible. You feel worse. You avoid thinking about it. You forget it again. You feel even worse. Round and round it goes.

The shame doesn't help. It doesn't make you remember better. It doesn't make you more organized. It just makes you feel terrible.

So how do we break it?

First, we need to separate the action from the identity. You forgot a bill. That doesn't make you irresponsible. It makes you human. It makes you someone with ADHD who struggles with executive function.

Second, we need systems that work with your brain, not against it. Automatic bill pay. Calendar reminders. Visual cues. Not because you're broken, but because your brain needs different support.

And third—and this is the hard one—we need to practice self-compassion.

I know. Self-compassion feels like making excuses. It feels like letting yourself off the hook. But it's not.

Self-compassion is saying: "I made a mistake. That's okay. Everyone makes mistakes. What can I learn from this? What system can I put in place to help next time?"

Shame says: "You are bad." Self-compassion says: "You did a hard thing with a brain that makes it harder. Let's figure out how to make it easier."

Here's something that helps me: I keep an "Evidence File." Every time I do something well—pay a bill on time, finish a project, remember an appointment—I write it down. Not to brag. But to have proof.

Because when I'm in the shame spiral, my brain lies to me. It says "You always mess up. You never get it right."

But the Evidence File doesn't lie. It says: "Actually, you paid twelve bills on time this year. You finished that big project last month. You remembered your friend's birthday."

🛠️ The 2-Minute Tool (8:00-10:00)

Today's Tool: Start Your Evidence File

Right now, open a note on your phone or grab a piece of paper. Write down three things you've done well recently.

They don't have to be big. "I made my bed." "I answered that email." "I showed up to work."

Save that note. Add to it whenever you do something—anything—well.

And next time you're in the shame spiral, read it. Let it be the antidote to the lie that you always mess up.

Because you don't always mess up. You mess up sometimes. And you do things right sometimes. And both are part of being human.

🎬 Outro (10:00-13:00)

You are not your mistakes. You are not your missed deadlines. You are not your forgotten appointments.

You are someone doing your best with a brain that makes things harder. And your best is enough.

The shame spiral wants you to believe you're broken. You're not. You're just different. And different isn't wrong.

So start that Evidence File. Be kind to yourself. And remember: Small steps count. Progress is progress. You're doing better than you think.

Thanks for listening to Focus & Flow. If this episode helped you, share it with someone who needs to hear it. And remember—you don't need to be perfect. You just need to keep going.

Until next time, keep flowing.
